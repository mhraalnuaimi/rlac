## Introduction
In this writing, I look at how the Large Language Model I chose, which is Gemini, interprets different visuals from my project. These visuals come from an analysis of violent vocabulary in Harry Potter novels and fanfiction from Assignment 1. I wanted to see how Gemini explains each visual with no context and how its explanations change once I tell it what the visuals represent. Comparing its first reactions with its later interpretations shows how much Gemini depends on context to understand meaning. It also helped me see how visuals can be misunderstood when viewed on their own.

## WORD CLOUD ANALYSIS

<img src="/rlac/assets/images/figure6.png">



<img src="/rlac/assets/images/wordcloud1.png"> 

*Figure 1: Word Cloud — Prompt 1: Explain what this visual shows. What do you think this data is about?*  

Gemini describes the word cloud as a picture showing which words appear most often. It notices that Harry, said, Draco, Gabriel, and Hermione are the largest words and guesses the text is Harry Potter fanfiction. It also points out that Gabriel is not a canon character. With no context, Gemini stays at a surface level and focuses only on word size and familiar names.

<img src="/rlac/assets/images/wordcloud2.png">

*Figure 2: Word Cloud — Prompt 2: What patterns or themes do you think this visual represents?* 

When asked about themes, Gemini looks deeper. It sees many family‑related words like *child*, *baby*, *parents*, and *cherub*, suggesting a domestic or family‑centered fanfiction. It also notices that dialogue words such as *said* and *asked* are very large, which makes it think the story is dialogue‑heavy. It also points out words describing expressions and body language, which suggests emotional interactions between characters.

<img src="/rlac/assets/images/wordcloud3.png">

*Figure 3: Word Cloud — Prompt 3: Interpret this visual again with the context of violent vocabulary analysis.*  

Once I give Gemini the real context, its interpretation shifts. It now notices that violent words are almost missing. It explains that this word cloud might represent a peaceful or low‑violence text used as a baseline in the project. Gentle words like *baby* and *cherub* stand out more than anything violent. With context, Gemini understands that the absence of violent vocabulary is the important part of this visual.

## TRENDS GRAPH ANALYSIS

<img src="/rlac/assets/images/trendgraph.png">



<img src="/rlac/assets/images/trendgraph1.png">

*Figure 4: Trends Graph — Prompt 1: Explain this visual based only on what you see.*

With no context, Gemini describes the structure of the graph. It explains that the y‑axis shows relative frequency and the x‑axis shows five documents. Each colored line represents a different word. It also notices background bars showing overall volume. At this point, Gemini can describe the graph but cannot explain why these words were chosen.

<img src="/rlac/assets/images/trendgraph2.png">

*Figure 5: Trends Graph — Prompt 2: What do you think this graph is measuring?*

Gemini begins to guess the purpose. It notices that the words being tracked relate to violence, such as *kill*, *blood*, *curse*, and *attack*. It also sees that the document names look like book titles. Based on this, it thinks the graph measures violent vocabulary across different texts. This shows how the LLM uses patterns to make an educated guess.

<img src="/rlac/assets/images/trendgraph3.png">

*Figure 6: Trends Graph — Prompt 3: Interpret this graph again with the context of violent word frequency.*

With context, Gemini becomes more accurate. It identifies which documents are canon and which are fanfiction. It explains that *Deathly Hallows* has the highest amount of violent vocabulary, which matches its darker tone. It also notices that some fanfiction stories use certain violent words more often than the canon books, such as a spike in the word *blood*. With context, Gemini understands that the graph compares how violence is written in different types of texts.

## HEATMAP ANALYSIS

<img src="/rlac/assets/images/figure2.png">



<img src="/rlac/assets/images/heatmap1.png">

*Figure 7: Heatmap — Prompt 1: Explain this heatmap with no context.*

Gemini describes the heatmap by explaining that the rows list violent words and the columns list five texts. Darker red means higher frequency. It notices that *Deathly Hallows* has a very dark cell for the word *death* and that some fanfiction texts have spikes in *blood* or *hurt*. Without context, Gemini can describe patterns but not their meaning.

<img src="/rlac/assets/images/heatmap2.png">

*Figure 8: Heatmap — Prompt 2: What patterns or themes do you think this visual represents?*

Gemini begins to interpret themes. It sees that *death* and *dead* appear more often than words like *killing* or *bleeding*, suggesting a focus on mortality rather than graphic action. It also notices that one fanfiction text uses the word *hurt* much more often, which suggests emotional or physical suffering. Gemini also points out that all texts avoid medical words like *bleeding* or *wounded*, showing a shared writing style.

<img src="/rlac/assets/images/heatmap3.png">

*Figure 9: Heatmap — Prompt 3: Interpret this heatmap again with the context of violent vocabulary analysis.*

With context, Gemini gives a clearer explanation. It sees that Rowling’s books focus more on the idea of death, especially in *Deathly Hallows*. Fanfiction focuses more on physical and emotional violence, such as *blood* and *hurt*. Gemini connects this to common fanfiction genres like *hurt/comfort*. It also notices that both canon and fanfiction avoid medical terms. With context, the LLM understands the deeper differences in how violence is written in these texts.

## Conclusion
Across all three visuals, I can see how much Gemini depends on context to understand data. Without context, it describes what it sees and makes guesses based on patterns. A visual on its own can be misunderstood, but with the right information, it becomes much easier to interpret. This issue isn’t just shown in my project; this also connects to one of our readings that we had in class, Pechenick et al., who warn that the Google Books corpus “readily conveys an illusion of large-scale cultural popularity.” Their point shows how easy it is to misread data when the context behind it is missing. I saw the same thing with Gemini, because without context it also made assumptions based only on surface patterns. With context, Gemini’s explanations become more accurate and meaningful. When context is added, the LLM moves beyond simple description and begins to connect the patterns to themes like mortality, emotional conflict, and writing style. This shift shows how important context is for turning raw visuals into real understanding.
